# Meraki — AI-Powered Big Feature Update Proposal

> **Premise**: Meraki already has a sophisticated AI backbone — on-device TFLite emotion classification, Groq-powered chat, Gemini journal analysis, and a long-term session memory system. This proposal is about *connecting these dots* and letting that intelligence surface everywhere in the app, not just inside the chatbot. The goal is to make every screen feel like it *knows* the user.

---

## The Core Philosophy Shift

The current app captures mood **reactively** — the user opens the app, drags a slider, and a number gets saved. The AI intelligence upgrade turns Meraki into a **proactive emotional companion** that understands patterns, anticipates needs, and surfaces insight at the right moment without being intrusive.

Everything proposed here is buildable on top of what already exists. No new AI infrastructure is needed — just deeper integration of the systems already in place.

---

## 1. Mood Log Screen — Complete Reimagination

### What It Is Now
A circular gradient slider (0–100) that the user drags to a number. One data point gets saved: a score and a timestamp.

### The Problem
A number from 0 to 100 is a blunt instrument. It captures *valence* (good vs. bad) but misses *why*, *energy level*, *intensity*, and nuance. Two people both at 45 could be exhausted-but-okay versus anxious-and-hiding-it. The AI can't distinguish them because the input is too shallow.

---

### Upgrade A — Conversational Quick-Log (Replace the Slider)

Instead of a slider, the new entry point is a floating bottom sheet that opens with a single warm question:

> *"Hey Ashad, how are you feeling right now?"*

The user types a few words — or even just one ("tired", "actually pretty okay", "ugh"). The on-device TFLite emotion classifier runs instantly on the input and maps it to a mood score + emotion category. The AI pre-fills the mood ring visually, and the user can nudge it if they disagree. A single tap confirms.

**What this unlocks:**
- The mood entry now has *language* attached to it, not just a number. That text feeds into pattern analysis.
- The TFLite classifier already does this for chat messages. Reusing it here is a natural extension.
- First-time users find it dramatically more intuitive than a numeric slider.
- The text snippet becomes a micro-journal entry, searchable and analyzable over time.

**Fallback**: Keep the circular selector as a "visual mode" toggle for users who prefer it. But make conversational the default.

---

### Upgrade B — Two-Axis Mood Capture (Valence + Energy)

Replace the single 0–100 axis with a **2D mood plane** (industry standard from affective computing):

- **X-axis**: Valence — Unpleasant ↔ Pleasant
- **Y-axis**: Arousal — Low Energy ↔ High Energy

The user places a single dot on the plane. This single tap captures four emotional quadrants:
- High energy + Pleasant = Excited / Happy
- Low energy + Pleasant = Calm / Content
- High energy + Unpleasant = Stressed / Anxious
- Low energy + Unpleasant = Sad / Depressed

**What this unlocks:**
- Two people both at "score 45" are now distinguishable: one is Sad (low-low), one is Anxious (high-low).
- The AI can give far more precise recommendations. A calm person needs different content than an anxious one at the same valence score.
- Internally, the valence axis maps directly to the existing 0–100 score for backward compatibility. Arousal becomes a new field.

---

### Upgrade C — Context-Aware Mood Prompts

The mood log screen adapts its opening question based on what the AI already knows:

- **Time of day**: Morning check-in ("Good morning — how are you starting the day?") vs. evening wind-down ("How did today end up feeling?")
- **Previous session memory**: If the last chat session flagged anxiety, the prompt shifts — "Yesterday felt a bit heavy. How are you today?"
- **Mood history patterns**: If the user consistently feels lower on Sundays, the Sunday prompt acknowledges it warmly.
- **Post-journal**: After saving a journal entry, automatically offer a mood log prompt with context from the journal's emotion tags pre-filled.

The MemoryManager and HomeViewModel's `dominantEmotion` flow already provide this data. This is purely a UI layer that reads existing state.

---

### Upgrade D — Mood Log Reasons (Smart Tagging)

After the quick-log, optionally offer 1–3 tap-to-select reason chips that are **dynamically generated by the AI based on the user's patterns**, not a static list:

- For a user who often mentions "work" in journals: chips like "Tough day at work", "Work went well", "Meeting stress"
- For a user who frequently mentions sleep: "Slept badly", "Rested"
- Generic fallbacks: "Social", "Health", "No specific reason"

These reason tags get attached to mood entries and become the most powerful input for the correlation engine (see Section 3). The Journal screen already does reason selection — this borrows that UX pattern and makes it smarter.

---

### Upgrade E — Voice Mood Entry

A microphone icon opens a 5-second voice clip. The audio is transcribed on-device (using Android's built-in SpeechRecognizer API, no cloud needed) and then fed through the TFLite classifier exactly like typed text. This is especially useful for users who capture moods while commuting or in bed.

## 2. Home Screen Mood Chart — Complete Reimagination

### What It Is Now
A card showing the last 7 mood entries as a vertical list: date | emoji | score | label. Static. No interactivity. No AI narrative.

### The Problem
A list of seven numbers with emojis gives no insight. The user can see they had a bad Wednesday but cannot understand *why*, whether it's a pattern, or what to do about it.

---

### Upgrade A — Replace the List with an Animated Insight Card

The `MoodLogsCard` becomes a **Living Mood Card** — a mini dashboard that cycles between three states with a swipe:

**State 1 — The Curve**: A smooth sparkline of the last 7 days with a colored gradient fill beneath it (already rendered beautifully on the MoodTracker screen — just bring it home). Color shifts dynamically: green-dominant weeks look warm, difficult weeks shift cooler. Tap any point to see the date and entry.

**State 2 — The AI Insight**: A single sentence generated by the AI summarizing the week's emotional pattern:
> *"You had a strong start this week but energy dipped mid-week. Journaling on Thursday seemed to help."*

This is generated by passing the week's mood scores + emotion logs + journal timestamps to Gemini (already integrated) with a compact prompt. Cached until the next mood log.

**State 3 — The Pattern Alert**: When the AI detects a meaningful pattern (3+ days of declining mood, a consistent low point on a specific day, a correlation between journaling and mood lift), it surfaces a single warm, non-alarming card:
> *"Noticed: You tend to feel more anxious on Sunday evenings. Want to try a short breathing exercise tonight?"*

The action button routes to Breathing or Chatbot depending on context.

---

### Upgrade B — Predictive Mood Forecast Line

Extend the mood chart with a **3-day forecast** rendered as a dashed continuation of the existing line, color-coded differently (e.g., soft white/grey vs the solid gradient). The forecast is based on:
- Weekday patterns from the last 30 days
- Current emotional trajectory (slope of last 3 entries)
- Session memory signals (ongoing stressors the chatbot is aware of)

This is a lightweight linear regression + weekday-average model, runnable entirely on-device. It doesn't need to be precise — it needs to prompt the user to *notice* their patterns and log intentionally.

The forecast line comes with a small label: *"Based on your patterns"*, keeping expectations honest.

---

### Upgrade C — Streak Meter Upgrade to "Momentum Score"

The current streak counter is a simple consecutive-days counter. Replace it with a **Momentum Score** — a composite that factors in:
- Mood log consistency (streak)
- Whether mood is trending upward or stable
- Journaling frequency
- Chatbot engagement

Displayed as a circular radial progress bar (0–100) with a label like "Strong Momentum" or "Building Momentum". The AI generates a one-line encouragement beneath it that changes weekly. This is more motivating than a raw number and less punishing when the streak breaks (the score degrades gracefully rather than resetting to zero).

---

### Upgrade D — Mood + Activity Correlation Badges

Below the chart, show 2–3 auto-detected correlation badges — small pill chips that the AI has identified from the user's history:

> 🔗 *Journaling → +12 pts avg next day*
> 🔗 *Sunday evenings → consistently lower*
> 🔗 *After chatting → mood lifts*

These are computed from the local Room database (EmotionLog + mood entries + journal timestamps). No cloud call needed. Tapping a badge opens a simple detail view with a mini chart showing the correlation visually.

## 3. New AI-Native Features (Enabled by the Upgrade)

These are entirely new features that become possible once the above infrastructure is in place.

---

### Feature — Proactive Check-In Nudges (Smarter Notifications)

The existing WorkManager notification system sends reminders. Upgrade it so notifications are *context-aware*:

- If the last 3 mood logs were declining and it's been 20+ hours since the last log: *"Checking in — how are you holding up today?"*
- If a journal entry mentioned a specific stressor (e.g., "big presentation tomorrow"): the next morning's notification references it — *"Hope the presentation went well. How are you feeling?"*
- If mood has been consistently high for 5+ days: *"You've had a great run this week. What's been helping?"* (positive reinforcement, routes to journal)

The MemoryManager's session summaries and mood trends together provide enough signal to make this feel genuinely personal rather than generic.

---

### Feature — Mood Reflection Mode (Weekly AI Narrative)

Every Sunday evening, the app generates a **Weekly Mood Story** — a short 3–4 sentence narrative written in warm, human language:

> *"This week had its ups and downs. Monday started strong, and your Wednesday journal seemed to help process some tension you were carrying. By Friday you'd found your footing. The consistent theme this week was work pressure — worth keeping an eye on."*

Delivered as a special card on the home screen that stays until dismissed. Generated by Gemini using the week's mood logs, emotion logs, and journal entry titles (not full content, for privacy). Cached in Room.

---

### Feature — AI Mood Journal Pairing

When the user logs a particularly low or high mood (e.g., below 30 or above 85), the app softly prompts:

> *"That's a significant moment. Want to capture what's behind it?"*

Tapping "Yes" opens the Journal screen with the mood pre-filled, the emotion category pre-selected, and a Gemini-generated opening prompt tailored to the mood:
- For low moods: *"What's been weighing on you?"*
- For high moods: *"What went right today? Let's capture it."*

This creates a natural flywheel: mood log → journal → AI analysis → richer session memory → better future insights.

---

### Feature — Emotional Weather Report (Home Screen Header)

Replace (or augment) the current quote card stack at the top of the home screen with an **Emotional Weather Report** — a personalized, AI-generated one-liner that changes daily based on the user's recent emotional data:

> ⛅ *"Mixed signals this week — some brightness coming through."*
> 🌧️ *"A heavier stretch. You're navigating it well."*
> ☀️ *"Clear and steady. Momentum is building."*

This is purely aesthetic and lightweight (generated once daily, cached), but it makes the home screen feel genuinely alive and personal rather than showing the same rotating quotes to everyone.

---

### Feature — Mood Insights Upgrade (Insights Screen)

The existing Insights screen shows basic analytics. Upgrade it to a full **Emotional Intelligence Dashboard** with three tabs:

- **Patterns**: AI-identified recurring patterns with mini visualizations (best day of week, worst time of day, longest positive streak, etc.)
- **Correlations**: What activities, days, or journal themes correlate with mood changes. Sortable by impact strength.
- **Growth**: Long-term emotional trajectory over 30/90 days. A simple question answered: "Is this person, on average, trending toward better wellbeing?" Shown honestly, without gamification pressure.

---

## 4. What to Keep Exactly As Is

Not everything needs to change. These features are already strong:

- **The Chatbot** — Groq streaming + session memory is excellent. The main improvement here is feeding more mood data *into* the chat context automatically.
- **The Breathing Screen** — Simple, functional, calming. Don't over-engineer it.
- **The Journal 3-Step Flow** — Emotion selection → reasons → reflection is a solid UX pattern. Enhance it with AI prompts, don't restructure it.
- **The Streak Counter concept** — Users are attached to streaks. Evolve it into Momentum Score rather than removing it entirely.

---

## 5. Implementation Priority Order

Given that these features build on each other, the recommended build order:

1. **Conversational Quick-Log** — Highest UX impact, reuses existing TFLite classifier directly. Immediate differentiation.
2. **Living Mood Card (Home Screen)** — The chart + AI insight sentence. Reuses Gemini integration already in place for journals.
3. **Context-Aware Notifications** — Builds on WorkManager + MemoryManager. High retention impact.
4. **Two-Axis Mood Capture** — Requires a new UI component and a DB schema addition (arousal field). Medium effort, high analytical value.
5. **Mood Reflection / Weekly Story** — Groq/Gemini prompt + caching. Moderate effort, high delight factor.
6. **Correlation Badges + Forecast Line** — Requires aggregation queries on Room. Build after the data model stabilizes.
7. **Insights Dashboard Rebuild** — Highest effort. Do last once the data from steps 1–4 is rich enough to analyze.

---

*This proposal is grounded in what Meraki already has. Every feature described here extends existing systems rather than introducing new ones. The TFLite classifier, Gemini integration, MemoryManager, Room database, and Groq API are all already present — this upgrade is about connecting them into a coherent, intelligent experience.*
